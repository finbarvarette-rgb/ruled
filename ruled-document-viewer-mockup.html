What is a demand letter?

A demand letter is a formal legal document that tells whoever owes you money exactly what they owe, why they need to pay, and the consequences if they don't respond within the deadline.

40% of cases resolve right here — before ever going to court. Send it, wait for the deadline, and see what happens.

Step 4 of 5
Serve the defendant.
After filing, you must formally notify the other party. Here's exactly how.
Why this matters: In small claims court, you are legally required to serve the defendant with a copy of your filed claim. If you don't serve them properly, the court cannot proceed with your case.
1
Get your filed claim documents
After you file at the courthouse, they will stamp your claim form and give you copies. You need at least one copy to serve on the defendant. Keep the originals for your records.
2
Choose your method of service
In Nova Scotia Small Claims Court you can serve by any of these methods:
Personal Service — Hand the documents directly to the defendant. Simplest and most reliable.
Registered Mail — Send by Canada Post registered mail to their last known address. Keep your tracking receipt.
Email — If the defendant has communicated with you by email previously, you may be able to serve by email. Check with the court clerk.
3
Complete your Proof of Service form
After serving, fill out the Proof of Service form (available at the courthouse or below). This confirms to the court that the defendant was properly notified.
4
File your Proof of Service
Return to the courthouse and file your completed Proof of Service. The court will then schedule your hearing date.
You typically have 30 days from filing your claim to serve the defendant. Do not delay.

Opening Statement
"Your Honour, my name is Finn Varette. I am the claimant in this matter.

In March 2026, I entered into an agreement with ABC Contracting Ltd. to repair and repaint the deck at my property. I paid a deposit of $1,200 upfront as agreed.

The defendant started the work but left it incomplete — removing several boards and then abandoning the job entirely. Over the following weeks, they stopped responding to my calls and messages.

I am here today seeking repayment of $2,400 — the full contract price — for work that was never completed and services that were not delivered as agreed.

I have payment records, text messages, and photographs of the incomplete work, which I will present as evidence."
Tip: Keep your opening under 3 minutes. State the facts clearly. Don't argue yet — save that for when you present your evidence.
Closing Statement
"Your Honour, I would ask you to consider the following.

I entered into a clear agreement with the defendant. I paid the agreed deposit in good faith. The defendant started the work, then abandoned it without explanation, and has refused to communicate or refund any money.

The evidence I have presented — the e-transfer records, the text message history, and the photographs of the incomplete work — demonstrates clearly that the defendant failed to deliver what was contracted.

I am not here seeking anything more than what I am owed. I respectfully ask that this court award me judgment in the amount of $2,400."
These are the arguments the defendant may raise and how to respond to each.
⚠ "The work was done but the claimant was unsatisfied."
Your response: Point to your photographs showing the deck with boards removed and work clearly incomplete. Ask the defendant to produce any evidence that the work was completed to the contracted standard.
⚠ "There was no written contract."
Your response: A verbal agreement is a legally binding contract in Nova Scotia. Present your text messages showing the agreed scope of work, price, and timeline. These messages confirm the agreement existed.
⚠ "The claimant caused delays or changed the scope."
Your response: Your text message history shows no such requests were made. Ask the defendant to provide specific dates and evidence of any changes you allegedly requested.
